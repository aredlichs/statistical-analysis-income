# Descriptive Statistical Analysis of Income Distribution

## Objective
The objective of this project is to apply descriptive statistics to analyze income distribution and demonstrate how different measures of central tendency and dispersion can lead to different interpretations of the same dataset.

This project was designed as an entry-level data analysis exercise, focusing on statistical reasoning rather than advanced tools.

## Dataset
A small synthetic dataset representing monthly income values was created to clearly illustrate the behavior of descriptive statistical measures, especially the impact of outliers on income analysis.

The dataset contains a limited number of observations by design, prioritizing clarity of interpretation over scale.

## Statistical Concepts Applied
The following descriptive statistics were calculated and analyzed:

- **Mean**: evaluated to demonstrate sensitivity to extreme values
- **Median**: used as a robust measure of central tendency
- **Mode**: analyzed to assess frequency concentration
- **Variance**: calculated to measure income dispersion
- **Standard Deviation**: used to quantify variability around the mean

All concepts were applied with an emphasis on interpretation rather than formula memorization.

## Key Findings
- The mean income is significantly influenced by high-income outliers, which can distort the perception of typical income.
- The median provides a more accurate representation of the income earned by the majority of individuals in a skewed distribution.
- The high variance and standard deviation indicate substantial dispersion, suggesting income inequality within the dataset.

These results highlight the importance of selecting appropriate statistical measures depending on data distribution characteristics.

## Limitations
- The dataset is small and synthetic, which limits real-world generalization.
- No inferential statistics were applied; the analysis is strictly descriptive.
- No automated tools (e.g., Python, SQL, Excel) were used in this version of the project.
